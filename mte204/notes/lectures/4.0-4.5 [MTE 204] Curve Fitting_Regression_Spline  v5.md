Polynomial interpolation – an nth order polynomial directly fits n+1 data points
(curve passes through all points).
If the underlying curve (actual response) is an nth order polynomial, a polynomial
based on n+1 data points will provide exact results (at the data points, and between
the data points).
If we are approximating a different underlying response (e.g. ln(x)) with a
polynomial), the polynomial will pass through the fitted data points, but will only
approximate the response between the data points.

4.5 Spline Interpolation [General]
A spline is a lower order polynomial applied to a subset of points in the data set.
Spline functions are characterized by the order of the fit.
For example, a 3rd order polynomial is called a ‘cubic spline’. This is the most widely used version in
engineering (provides continuity of 1st and 2nd derivative).
The concept originated from using a
thin, flexible strip of material to join
points (e.g. pins in a board).
This is called a natural spine. Note that
the slope of the flexible strip is
constant at the end points.

4.5 Spline Interpolation [Cubic or third-order, aka Hermite spline]
Cubic splines (section 18.6.5)
Three commonly used end conditions:
1. Natural spline (second derivatives = 0 at end points)
(straightens at ends due to 2nd derivative = 0)
2. Clamped end condition (first derivatives = 0 at the end points)
(levels off to zero slope at ends)
3. Not-a-knot end condition (force continuity of the third
derivative at the second and next-to-last knots).
(non-zero 2nd derivatives, exhibits more curvature)
Hermite spline: cubic polynomial with continuity in function values and first derivatives
Commonly used in interpolation of data.
